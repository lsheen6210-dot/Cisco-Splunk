Mapping the Cisco-to-Splunk Data Pipeline: 40 Products, 10 Apps, One Platform

How the industry’s broadest infrastructure portfolio feeds the industry’s most versatile data platform

By Matt Poland

If you operate a network built on Cisco, you’re sitting on a goldmine of machine data—telemetry from firewalls, switches, routers, identity engines, endpoint agents, cloud security services, and more. The question isn’t whether that data has value. The question is whether you’re capturing it, correlating it, and turning it into action. That’s where Splunk comes in.

Why We Built This Diagram

One of the most common questions we hear from Cisco customers evaluating Splunk: “Which of my Cisco products can send data to Splunk, and how?”  The answer turns out to be surprisingly broad. We mapped 40 distinct Cisco products to the 10 supported Splunk Apps and Technology Add-ons that ingest their data, and the result is the interactive Sankey diagram shown below.

Hover over any Cisco product on the left to see exactly which Splunk app receives its data. Hover over a Splunk app on the right to see every Cisco product it supports. Click any app to jump straight to its Splunkbase listing.

Security: The Flagship Use Case

The most striking cluster in the diagram is the Cisco Security Cloud app, which ingests data from 16 products—everything from Duo and Secure Endpoint to XDR, Secure Firewall, AI Defense, and Identity Intelligence. For security teams, this represents a single pane of glass across network, endpoint, identity, email, and cloud workload telemetry. Splunk Enterprise Security layers risk-based alerting, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, and automated investigation workflows on top of this data, turning raw logs into detections and detections into response.

Observability: From the Data Center to the Edge

The second major cluster is the Cisco Catalyst Add-on for Splunk, mapping 11 products that span the full networking stack: access points, switches, SD-WAN, ISE, Meraki, wireless controllers, and even Cyber Vision for OT environments. Combined with the Cisco DC Networking add-on (ACI, Nexus Switches, Nexus Dashboard) and Cisco ThousandEyes for internet and application-path visibility, Splunk becomes a comprehensive observability layer across campus, WAN, data center, and cloud edge. IT operations teams can correlate network health, application performance, and user experience data in a single platform—cutting mean time to resolution and making proactive capacity planning a reality rather than an aspiration.

Beyond Security and Observability: Splunk as a Data Platform

Here’s the part that often gets overlooked. Splunk is not a SIEM that happens to do other things. It’s a data platform that happens to be very good at security. The same Search Processing Language that powers threat hunting can drive network capacity planning, license utilization tracking, compliance auditing, cost analytics, and operational intelligence across any domain that generates machine data.

Think about what’s possible when all 40 of these data sources land in one platform: a compliance team auditing ISE authentication policies alongside Duo MFA enforcement; a network engineering team correlating SD-WAN performance with Meraki wireless metrics; a cloud architect tracking multi-cloud defense posture alongside UCS compute utilization. These are workflows customers build every day on Splunk because the data is already there.

The versatility of the platform means that the integration investment you make for security pays dividends across IT operations, compliance, executive reporting, and emerging use cases like AI governance and agentic system monitoring. While this diagram focuses on Cisco products, Splunk can ingest any text based data from any product.
